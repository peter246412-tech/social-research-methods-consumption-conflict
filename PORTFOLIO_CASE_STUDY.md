# Portfolio Case Study

## Project Title

Hyper-Connected Society and Precautionary Consumption: A Social Research Design on U.S.-Iran Conflict and South Korean Household Spending

## Context

This project was completed as part of a Social Research Methods team assignment. The assignment required us to move beyond choosing an interesting topic and instead build a researchable social-science problem with clear concepts, measurable variables, hypotheses, data sources, and validity considerations.

Our team focused on how overseas conflict may affect domestic consumer behavior in a hyper-connected media environment. The topic was designed to connect international politics, media exposure, consumer psychology, and household economic behavior.

## Problem

International conflicts are often analyzed at the level of diplomacy, national security, or macroeconomic indicators. However, in a highly connected society, conflict information reaches ordinary citizens immediately through search engines, news alerts, and social media.

The research problem was whether this information environment changes household behavior, especially precautionary consumption of essential goods.

## Research Question

Does the current hyper-connected information environment make South Korean households respond more strongly to overseas conflict than they did in earlier periods?

## My Contribution

I helped structure the project as a social-science research design rather than a broad issue report. The work included:

- Refining the topic into a testable research question
- Defining independent, dependent, mediating, and control variables
- Framing essential-goods consumption as a measurable behavioral response
- Connecting public datasets to each variable
- Considering internal validity and construct validity threats
- Building a defensible logic for why media exposure and search interest could mediate consumer response

## Methodological Design

The study was designed as a secondary-data comparative analysis.

Key components:

- Compare older and current U.S.-Iran conflict periods
- Observe changes in household essential-goods expenditure around conflict events
- Use CPI, exchange rate, fuel price, and other macroeconomic indicators as controls
- Use news volume, search trends, and consumer sentiment as proxy indicators for information exposure and public anxiety
- Apply Difference-in-Differences and time-series logic as possible empirical strategies

## Why This Project Matters

This project demonstrates how social science can make abstract social changes measurable. "Anxiety about overseas war" is difficult to observe directly, but the project shows how researchers can approximate it through consumption data, search behavior, news volume, and consumer sentiment.

For my broader portfolio, this project complements technical automation work by showing the social-science side of my background: asking researchable questions, designing evidence-based analysis, and connecting data to real social behavior.
